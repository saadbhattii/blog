# Befriending Imaginary Numbers

In its simplest form, quantum computing is just doing matrix multiplication with complex numbers. Hence, getting comfortable with complex numbers is what we should strive for. Let's take that first step today and talk about imaginary numbers.

First of all, calling them "imaginary numbers" doesn't help, as these numbers are not imaginary at all. For the sake of this post, I am going to call them iota numbers instead.

Let's not ghost them this time, and just get to know them. They are not weirdos, neither are they outliers. But you may ask, how can I befriend a number that is not even real? Well, are negative numbers real? Really? Have you ever questioned negative numbers or how whimsical they sound when you describe what they really are? The math checked out since you were a kid, did you ever need a philosophical reasoning for it? No. The truth is iota numbers are as "real" as the negative numbers, zero, fractions, or what you refer as the real numbers. If you believe in these numbers, then iota numbers are the same. Let's not discriminate! Iota numbers matter.

Let's go through a bit of history. Humanity became aware at some point that numbers can help you be precise about things, and reliance on language alone made things ambiguous, hence mathematics became a tool to quantify everything around us, such that we could predict the future or solve problems. Humanity became obsessed with modeling reality into mathematical models starting with things they could see at night, aka, astronomy. Tracking planets, or the sun to predict seasons or whatever. This need for mathematics drove us to develop our mathematical toolkit. All sorts of numbers were invented for the sake of updating our mathematical toolkit to solve a problem.

First came the invention of whole numbers such that we could count the number of sheep someone had or the amount of fruits you exchanged. Then came the tax system, it introduced the idea of debt, we invented negative numbers to represent the amount of stuff someone owed and the math worked out. For having justice in taxes, we came up with percentages, then to be accurate and avoid errors, we came up with decimals, and then zero came along as well. This simplified history is not in a chronological order but it gives us a good understanding on how and why numbers evolved. Whenever we encountered a mathematical problem that couldn't be solved by our existing toolkit, we were forced to invent. 

Around 500 years ago we encountered a problem, the square root of -1, which meant that what number when multiplied by itself will give -1? 

In school, we are taught:

```
a. +1 * +1 = +1
b. -1 * -1 = +1
c. -1 * +1 = -1
```

You can see clearly that there is no number in this toolkit which when multiplied by itself gives -1. (-1 and +1 are different numbers obviously). There was no: 

```
d. x * x = -1
```

So they decided to add this in the toolkit to solve the problem. That's literally what happened, but instead of writing "x", they wrote "i": 

```
d. i * i = -1
```

Which meant that the square root of -1 was i. That's literally it. That's what solved the problem and the mathematics checked out. That's how all types of numbers were invented; out of necessity. Hence you got the "imaginary" numbers. Brilliant but yet terrible naming. Gauss preferred to call them "lateral numbers", I will discuss in the future why that is. Until then you can call them ghost numbers, as that is a lot cooler.
